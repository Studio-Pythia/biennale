Venice Biennale · 61st International Art Exhibition · 9 May – 22 November 2026

Every Pavilion is a State Op

A financing-and-architecture map of the 2026 Biennale. Who pays, who picks, who shows. The art comes second.

By Russ Jones · 10 May 2026

The 2026 Biennale will be remembered for what it stopped doing. The Golden Lion was abolished in late April after the international jury resigned over Russia and Israel being allowed to compete. Italy's own Culture Minister boycotted the opening for the first time in the institution's 131-year history. On 8 May the cultural workers walked off — also a first. The European Commission cut €2 million from the Foundation. By the time the public was let in on 9 May, the only prize left was a public email vote announced on closing day.

This is the year to stop talking about the art and start looking at the wiring. Every state pavilion is a state op. The British Council pays the British Council's curator to pick a British artist to mount British self-criticism in a Giardini building owned by Britain. Russia's commissioner is the daughter of a deputy director of Rostec, the state defence conglomerate. The US pavilion is handled by a brand-new nonprofit founded after the Mellon and Ford Foundations stopped showing up, funded by Trump's Secretary of the Navy. Austria's bathroom-fixture sponsor, Laufen, is literally embedded in the artwork.

The interesting question isn't "art or propaganda?" It's: which of these governance shapes can survive the attention economy that the institution itself has now collapsed back into? Three archetypes are visible. Austria ran a meritocratic open call — fifty-four submissions, three-stage expert jury, minister rubber-stamps. Output: SEAWORLD VENICE, a flooded pavilion with naked performers tolling a salvaged church bell, a closed-loop sewage tank fed by the Biennale's own toilets, two-hour queues. The British Council has been picking the British artist itself for seventy-plus years; the 2026 commission went to a 71-year-old Turner Prize laureate, funded jointly by the Council, Frieze, and Cork Street Galleries — and yes, Frieze is the same magazine that published an op-ed in February 2025 arguing the pavilion should go to a younger artist. The Holy See signed a three-year deal with Intesa Sanpaolo in 2024 covering the next two Biennales, freeing its curators (Hans Ulrich Obrist, Ben Vickers, Soundwalk Collective this year) from chasing money and letting them take over a hidden Carmelite garden in Cannaregio with Brian Eno, FKA twigs and Patti Smith.

Below: every participating pavilion, what it cost, and where the money came from. Lay the architecture next to the money and the output stops being a mystery.

The donor web

What you cannot see by walking the Giardini is who keeps showing up across multiple pavilions. Move around the graph. Lines connect a funder to every pavilion it backs, and an artist to the commercial gallery that represents them. Where a gallery also funds the pavilion of its own artist — Galerie Thaddaeus Ropac for Florentina Holzinger at Austria — the triangle closes. Where a luxury house, a bank, or a state-aligned individual funds in multiple places, the constellation is the soft-power apparatus that the discrete pavilion view obscures.

Where to draw the line

The May 8 strike's central demand was the removal of Israel's pavilion. The jury that resigned tried to bar Russia and Israel from award consideration on the grounds that their leaders are under ICC investigation. The Foundation overruled them — citing inclusion — and replaced the prize with a public vote nobody on the jury would put their name to. Buttafuoco's defence: any country with diplomatic relations with Italy can show up.

The honest position is that there isn't one. Once you admit every pavilion is a state op, drawing the line at war or genocide is a moral choice you have to argue, not a structural conclusion you can read off the wiring. Russia's pavilion is a Rostec-adjacent rave. Britain's is a Turner Prize laureate diagnosing British loathing of the Other, funded by an art fair that earlier called for the commission to go elsewhere. The US is an apolitical sculptor commissioned by a Mar-a-Lago-connected nonprofit. The Holy See is a Patti Smith inauguration in a Carmelite garden, paid for by Italy's largest bank on a three-year deal. Austria is a flooded pavilion with a sewage tank funded partly by the artist's own dealer.

If you think one of these is meaningfully different in kind from the others, the wiring is what you have to defend. The map is here so you can see what you're defending.

Methodology & uncertainties

Data was assembled in early May 2026 from official pavilion sites, commissioning institutions' press releases, the Biennale's own catalogue at labiennale.org, and reporting in Artnet, Frieze, ArtReview, Hyperallergic, Artforum, Apollo, e-flux and the relevant national press for each country. Most pavilions do not publicly disclose total budgets — Italy is one of the few that does. Where a budget cell is empty, treat that as a finding, not an absence: opacity is part of what the map shows.

Donor-pavilion edges are drawn only where named in primary or reputable secondary sources. Where a name appeared in only one source or only at a benefit event, it is annotated as such. The "upside-down crucifix" detail in the Pussy Riot intervention at the Russian Pavilion could not be verified across sources and is not represented. The international jury chair Solange Farkas, members Zoe Butt, Elvira Dyangani Ose, Marta Kuzma and Giovanna Zapperi resigned together on 30 April 2026.

Three deaths shape the edition: Koyo Kouoh (1967–2025), curator of the main exhibition In Minor Keys, died May 2025 of liver cancer before installing the show; Henrike Naumann (1984–2026), one of two German Pavilion artists, died February 2026; Alexander Kluge (1932–2026), filmmaker, died earlier in 2026 — his final twelve-station film is in the Holy See pavilion.
